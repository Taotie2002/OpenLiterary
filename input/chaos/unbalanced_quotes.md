"Tell me," she said, "about the Shrike."

He was silent for a long moment. The wind howled outside the tent. The candles guttered in their holders.

"The Shrike is a god of metal and pain," he said finally. "It walks between the seconds. It remembers every death. It forgets every life. It grants one wish to each pilgrim, and it takes the wish back with interest.

"Does it have a name? A true name?"

"No one knows its true name. The pilgrims call it the Lord of Pain. The TechnoCore calls it a future. The Ousters call it a memory. The Church calls it a test.

"And what do you call it?"

He looked at her then, and his eyes were the eyes of a man who had looked into a fire and seen something that did not look back.

"I call it what it is," he said. "The end of poetry. The end of allusion. The end of all the borrowed music we have used to fill the silence of our lives.

"And yet you climb."

"Yes," he said. "And yet I climb.

"Because of the wish?"

"Because of the wish. Because of the woman I lost in the burning place. Because of the verses I have not yet written. Because the climb is all I have left, and I will not stop climbing until the Shrike stops me, or until I have climbed so high that the climb itself becomes the destination.

"Is that not what pilgrims do?"

"Is that not what all of us do?" she asked, and her voice was soft, and the candles were burning low, and the wind was carrying the scent of ozone and ancient dust across the plateau where they had made their camp on the eve of the pilgrimage. He did not answer. He looked at the fire, and the fire looked back at him, and in the fire he saw the face of the woman he had loved, and the face of the god he had come to kill, and the face of the child he had never had, and the face of the man he had been before the war, and the faces were all the same face, and the face was his own, and the fire burned, and the wind howled, and the Shrike counted heartbeats in the dark.
