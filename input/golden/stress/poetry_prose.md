## I. The Lover's Complaint

She walks in beauty, like the night

of cloudless climes and starry skies,

and all that's best of dark and bright

meets in her aspect and her eyes...

***

We are not ourselves when we are in love. We are borrowed things — borrowing the heart from a Greek poet, the eyes from an Italian master, the sigh from a Persian lover who died before Christ was born. The lover is a kind of thief. The lover steals the songs that were never his and makes them his own, and the original poets turn in their graves, and the songs continue.

She walks in beauty. He walks in grief. I walk in the rain, which is neither beauty nor grief but something in between, something that poets have tried to name for three thousand years and have not yet named.

***

## II. The Poet at Forty

When I was young, I wrote of roses.

When I was thirty, I wrote of thorns.

When I was forty, I wrote of neither.

When I was fifty, I wrote of silence.

Now I am old, and the silence writes me...

***

## III. Letters to a Dead Poet

Dear Keats,

I am writing to you from a garden

where the nightingales have all gone deaf.

I have tried to sing your songs.

I have failed at every note.

Forgive me. The voice is not what it was.

Yours, in grief,

— A lesser poet

***

Dear Shakespeare,

I am writing to you from a stage

where the actors have all forgotten

which play they are in.

I have tried to play your parts.

I have bungled every entrance.

Forgive me. The body is not what it was.

Yours, in admiration,

— A lesser player

***

Dear Homer,

I am writing to you from a shore

where the ships have all gone silent.

I have tried to count the oars.

I have lost the count at three.

Forgive me. The wine is not what it was.

Yours, in awe,

— A lesser singer

***

Dear Dante,

I am writing to you from a circle

where the damned have all gone home.

I have tried to map the pit.

I have sketched the first descent.

Forgive me. The compass is not what it was.

Yours, in flame,

— A lesser cartographer

***

## IV. The Final Verse

Beauty is truth, truth beauty.

That is all

Ye know on earth,

and all ye need to know.

***

— after Keats, after Shakespeare, after all of them
