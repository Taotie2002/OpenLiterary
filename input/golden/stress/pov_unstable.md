# Stress Set B: Unstable POV (Narrative Voice Shifts)

**Target feature**: `pov_markers` → high `pov_changes` → `base_ratio -= 0.1`
**Expected**: POV changes > 20; author_priority_ratio ≈ 0.6 (clamped)

***

I walked into the room. I had not been there before. I knew, somehow, that I would never leave it the same way I had entered. My heart was pounding in my chest. I tried to remember why I had come. I could not. I sat down at the table. I waited for her to speak.

She spoke. She told me her name, though I had not asked for it. She told me her story, though I had not asked for that either. She told me that she had loved me once, in a life I did not remember, in a city that no longer existed. She told me that the war had taken everything from her, and that she had taken everything from me, and that we were even now, even if we were not equal.

***

He was not listening. He had stopped listening the moment she had said her name. He was thinking of the war. He was thinking of the city. He was thinking of the long walk through the ruins, the smell of smoke and ash, the sound of his own boots on broken glass. He had been a different man then. He had been someone who believed in things. He had believed in her. He had believed in the cause. He had believed in the future.

Now he believed in nothing. He sat across from her and believed in nothing.

***

We had met, she reminded him, on a night like this one. The rain had been falling for three days. The river had flooded. The bridges were out. We had taken shelter in the church on the hill. We had been the only ones there. The priest had gone home. The candles had burned down to stubs. We had talked until dawn.

I remember, he said. I remember all of it. I remember the candles. I remember the rain. I remember the way you looked at me in the morning light.

And I remember, she said, the way you stopped looking.

***

We were both silent for a long time. The room was very quiet. The light had changed. Outside, the wind had picked up, and the trees were bending under its weight. Inside, the clock ticked, and the fire burned low, and we sat across from each other like two people who had once been one person and were now trying to remember how.

We had been young once. We had believed in things once. We had loved each other once, in the way that young people love, completely and without reservation. Now we were old, and we believed in nothing, and we loved each other only in the way that old people love, cautiously and with the constant awareness that the other might be about to disappear.

***

She told him about the child. He had not known there was a child. She told him the child had been born in the third year of the war, in a refugee camp near the river, in a tent that leaked when it rained. She told him the child had been a girl. She told him she had named the girl after his mother.

He did not ask what had happened to the child. He did not need to ask. He could see it in her face. He could see it in the way she held her hands, in the way she did not look at him when she spoke, in the way her voice caught on certain syllables.

The child was gone. The child was part of the war, the way the bridges were part of the war, the way the churches and the candles and the long nights were part of the war. The child had been a casualty, and casualties do not come back.

***

I told her I was sorry. I had not meant to say it. The words came out before I could stop them. She looked at me then, really looked at me, for the first time since she had begun to speak. She saw, I think, that I was sorry. She saw, I think, that I had always been sorry, even when I did not know what I was sorry for.

He reached across the table. He took her hand in his. His hand was cold. Her hand was warm. He held on to the warmth the way a drowning man holds on to a piece of wreckage.

We sat like that for a long time. The fire burned down to embers. The clock ticked. The wind howled outside. And we held on to each other, two people who had once been one person, two people who had been broken apart by a war they did not start and could not stop, two people who were trying now, in this quiet room, to remember how to be one person again.
