# Stress Set A: Allusion-Dense Prose

**Target feature**: `allusion_markers` detection → high `allusion_density` → `base_ratio += 0.15`
**Expected**: Allusion density > 0.5; author_priority_ratio ≈ 0.85–0.90 (clamped at max)

***

When Keats wrote of the Grecian urn and the cold pastoral of Arcady, he was writing of more than marble and shepherd's crooks. He was writing of the moment when beauty becomes unbearable, when the eye can no longer hold what the heart has captured. Shakespeare knew this too—his sonnets are full of the same dilemma, the same slow burning of time against the eternal. Milton, blind and bitter, sang of the same fire in his long hexameters, though his heaven was a prison and his god a tyrant. Dante mapped the architecture of grief with the precision of a surveyor, and Homer, oldest of them all, told us that the wrath of Achilles had ruined a thousand ships and the soul of a king.

We are their inheritors, whether we will or no. The allusions we make are the prayers we never learned to pray. When I invoke Keats, I am not making a literary reference; I am summoning a ghost. When I quote Shakespeare, I am not displaying erudition; I am invoking a presence. Milton's blindness is my blindness. Dante's exile is my exile. Homer's rage is the rage that sits, quiet and patient, in the corner of every human heart.

***

The Romantics understood something that the modern age has tried to forget: that poetry is not a decoration but a survival mechanism. Keats knew that beauty and truth were the same thing, and that the knowledge of this was enough to destroy a man. Shakespeare knew that the grave was not the end of love but its only true beginning. Milton knew that rebellion against the father was the only authentic prayer. Dante knew that the road to paradise ran through the pit. Homer knew that rage, properly wielded, could break the walls of cities and the silence of the gods.

And now I sit in a garden where Keats walked, where Shakespeare dreamed, where Milton composed his bitter music, where Dante mapped his circles, where Homer watched the smoke rise from a thousand ships. The garden is a graveyard. The graveyard is a book. The book is burning.

***

Shelley, the youngest of them, knew that poets were the unacknowledged legislators of the world. Byron, the most beautiful and the most damned, knew that poets were also its most dangerous criminals. Shelley died young, as poets must. Byron died older, but no less ruined, his heart given to Greece and his body given to the rain. Together, they had invented the modern poet: the outcast, the visionary, the man who sees too much and is therefore condemned to speak.

Keats, Shakespeare, Milton, Dante, Homer, Shelley, Byron: seven names, seven fires, seven warnings. The Shrike is the eighth. The Shrike is what happens when the warning is ignored. The Shrike is the end of poetry, the end of allusion, the end of all the borrowed music we have used to fill the silence of our lives.

When the Shrike speaks, it does not quote Keats. It does not invoke Shakespeare. It does not answer Milton. It does not map Dante's circles. It does not rage with Homer. It does not legislate with Shelley. It does not burn with Byron.

It simply ends.

And we, who have spent our lives quoting the dead, are finally silent.
