# Chapter 1: The Shrike

The Shrike is not a god, nor a demon, nor even a machine in the sense that we understand the word. It is a creature of pain and mystery, a figure of jagged steel and chrome that walks between the seconds of our lives.

To the pilgrims, the Shrike is the Lord of Pain. They come to the Time Tombs not to worship, but to seek an end—some to find a miracle, others to find a swift and bloody death. I stood at the edge of the plateau, watching the iron-grey sky churn above the spires of the tombs. My thoughts turned to the words of Keats, the poet who, like us, lived in the shadow of mortality and the cruel beauty of eternal art.

"Beauty is truth, truth beauty," I whispered to the wind, wondering if the Shrike knew the truth of its own metal heart. It moved in the shadows, a serrated silhouette against the horizon, its multiple eyes reflecting the cold, indifferent stars.

The pilgrims were a strange lot: a priest, a soldier, a poet, a scholar, a detective, a consul's daughter, and the template of a cybrid. Seven souls bound for the Time Tombs, each carrying their own private hell. Father Lenar Hoyt, his cruciform throbbing with the rhythm of a resurrection he could not explain. Colonel Fedmahn Kassad, dreaming of the woman who walked through fire and called herself Moneta. Martin Silenus, the ancient poet who had sold his soul for verses that would outlive the stars.

We walked in silence, the crunch of volcanic ash beneath our boots the only sound. The Time Tombs loomed ahead, vast and inexplicable, their anti-entropic fields shimmering like heat haze. Somewhere inside, the Shrike waited. It always waited.

"Remember," the consul's voice crackled over the comm, "the Shrike grants one wish to the pilgrim who reaches it. The others... the others become part of its tree of thorns."

A tree of thorns. I had seen the holograms—impaled bodies stretching toward a steel sky, screaming in eternal ecstasy. The Shrike's garden. Moneta had shown me, in the sim-space where time ran backwards and forwards both.

"We are not here to die," Silenus muttered, his voice a dry rasp. "We are here to witness. To record. That is the poet's burden."

"Speak for yourself," Kassad replied, his hand resting on the pulse rifle at his hip. "I came to kill a god."

The wind shifted, carrying the scent of ozone and ancient dust. Above us, the moons of Hyperion rose—one whole, one shattered. The labyrinth of the Time Tombs beckoned, and somewhere in its heart, the Shrike counted heartbeats.

# Chapter 2: The Priest's Tale

Father Lenar Hoyt knelt in the chapel of the starship *Tree of Pain*, the cruciform on his chest pulsing in time with a heartbeat that was not his own. The pain was familiar now—seven years since Father Duré had died in the Bikura village, seven years since the parasite had claimed him as its new host.

"Father?" The acolyte's voice was hesitant. "The pilgrimage is announced. The Shrike... the Time Tombs..."

Hoyt rose slowly, his robes brushing the cold deck. "Yes. I know. The Church has confirmed it. The Ousters mass at the system's edge, the TechnoCore calculates probabilities no human can comprehend, and the Shrike walks again."

He had seen the Shrike once, in the caves beneath the Bikura village. Not the god the villagers worshipped, but the thing that fed on their pain. The cruciform had saved him then—resurrecting him, binding him to a cycle of death and rebirth that made him immortal and damned.

"The Pope wants me to go," Hoyt said, more to himself than the acolyte. "To witness. To report. As if my soul weren't already forfeit."

The starship's viewport showed Hyperion growing larger, a jewel of green and blue marred by the Time Tombs' anomaly. Somewhere down there, the answers waited. Or perhaps only more questions.

***

The Bikura had been a simple people, gentle and untouched by the Hegemony's wars. They had welcomed Duré and Hoyt with bread and song, not knowing that the cruciform parasite had ridden in the priest's blood. The village elder had smiled when he saw the cross on Hoyt's chest.

"You carry our god inside you," he had said.

"I carry the symbol of my god," Hoyt had replied. "Your god is something else."

And the elder had laughed, a sound like bells in a ruined chapel. "All gods are something else, Father. That is what makes them gods."

***

The pilgrimage, Hoyt had learned in his years of study, was older than the Church. It predated the Pax, predated the Hegemony, predated humanity's first tentative steps into the spiral arm. The Shrike had always called pilgrims to the Tombs. The only thing that had changed was the language in which the calling was heard.

"The cruciform is not a gift," Hoyt whispered to the dark of his cabin. "It is a leash. The Shrike put it on me when I was too weak to refuse. And now it pulls, and I follow, and the Church believes I follow for love."

He touched the cross on his chest. It pulsed with the rhythm of a heart that had stopped seven years ago and had refused, ever since, to stay stopped.

***

"I am afraid," Hoyt told the consul on the eve of the threshold. "I am afraid of what I will see. I am afraid of what the Shrike will show me. I am afraid that I will see myself on the tree of thorns, and that I will recognize myself, and that I will know that I deserved it."

The consul, who had seen his own death in a hundred simulations, said nothing. There was nothing to say. They were all afraid. The pilgrimage was not for the brave. It was for the doomed.

# Chapter 3: The Soldier's Tale

Colonel Fedmahn Kassad had killed for the Hegemony in thirty-seven worlds. He had killed for love in two. He had killed for grief in one. And now, on the eve of the pilgrimage, he sat in the observation lounge and tried to remember the face of the woman he had loved.

She had called herself Moneta. In the old language of Old Earth, it meant "she who warns." She had warned him, in the burning place, of what was to come. He had not listened. He had followed her into the fire anyway, because that is what soldiers do when love is louder than reason.

The fire had not killed him. It had changed him. When he had crawled out, his skin was unmarked but his dreams were full of thorns.

***

"The Shrike is not a creature," Moneta had told him in the burning place, before the fire. "The Shrike is a future. A future the universe is trying to remember, the way a man dying of thirst remembers water."

"And what future does it remember?"

"The future where everything ends. Not with a war, not with a plague, not with the cooling of the stars. With a single act of will, performed by a single creature that has forgotten how to be anything except an ending."

"Could such a creature be killed?"

Kassad had asked the question with the coldness of a soldier. Moneta had answered with the coldness of a god.

"Could an ending be killed?"

***

In the Ouster wars, Kassad had learned to fight without hope. Hope was a luxury the FORCE:ground troops could not afford. They fought because they were ordered to fight. They died because the enemy was stronger. They won when the enemy's logistics failed and lost when their own did. Hope had nothing to do with it.

But in the burning place, with Moneta's hand in his, he had felt something he had not felt since childhood. He had felt hope.

"The fire will not kill you," she had said. "The fire will make you what the Shrike needs you to be. A witness. A soldier. A lover. A killer. All of these things, held together by the memory of a face you cannot forget."

"And if I refuse?"

"You will not refuse. You have already followed me into the fire. The refusal is past. The future is the only thing left."

***

In the morning, Kassad walked to the gate with the others. The pilgrim's path was narrow. The Shrike's shadow fell across it. He did not look back. He had learned, in a hundred wars, that looking back was a way of dying twice.

Moneta waited somewhere ahead, in the place where time ran backwards, in the garden of thorns. He would find her, or she would find him. The outcome did not matter. What mattered was the finding.

# Chapter 4: The Poet's Tale

Martin Silenus had not written a poem in eleven years. The words had dried up somewhere around the time of his third divorce and his fourth liver transplant, and he had not been able to find them since. He wrote novels now—long, sprawling, unreadable novels that critics called "ambitious" and readers called "unreadable." He was rich. He was famous. He was the most miserable man in the Hegemony.

"Poetry is the memory of what we have lost," he told the cybrid template one night, when the stars were bright and the wine was cheap. "When we lose the ability to feel, we lose the ability to write. When we lose the ability to write, we lose the ability to remember. And when we lose the ability to remember, we are no longer human. We are merely things that move."

"You speak of the Shrike," the template said.

"I speak of myself. The Shrike is only the most obvious example. We are all becoming Shrikes, in our small ways. Cutting away the parts of ourselves that feel. Sharpening the parts that cut."

"And the pilgrimage?"

"The pilgrimage is a way of remembering. One last chance to feel what we have forgotten how to feel. One last chance to write a poem that someone will actually read."

***

Silenus had walked this path a thousand times in sim-space. He had walked it with Keats in the forests of Hampstead, with Shakespeare in the alleys of London, with Homer on the shores of the wine-dark sea. He had walked it alone, in the dark, in the years after the words had dried up.

Now he walked it for real. And the difference, he discovered, was the difference between memory and presence. Memory is a kind of ghost. Presence is a kind of god.

"What will you write," asked the consul, "when we reach the tombs?"

"Nothing," said Silenus. "I will not need to write. The Tombs will write themselves on my soul. And when I die, the poems will walk out of my grave and find their own voices."

***

In the morning, he climbed with the others. He recited fragments of Endymion as he walked, and the basalt columns of the Tombs seemed to lean closer to listen. Keats had written of pain and beauty. Silenus had lived them both. Now he walked into the place where they met.

"Beauty is truth," he whispered, and the wind carried the words into the labyrinth, where the Shrike waited.

***

# Chapter 5: The Detective's Tale

The woman they called Lamia had been a detective for the TechnoCore for as long as she could remember, and before that she had been a sim-stim construct designed to be the perfect companion for the perfect client, and before that she had been a sequence of code in a wetware core that was not quite a brain and not quite a machine. She did not know what she had been before the code. She suspected she had been nothing. She suspected the code was her soul.

"The TechnoCore sent you to watch us," the consul said, on the eve of the pilgrimage.

"The TechnoCore sent me to record you," Lamia replied. "There is a difference. Watching implies judgment. Recording implies only memory."

"And what will the TechnoCore do with the memory?"

"Feed it to the Void Which Binds. The Core believes the Void is the substrate of all consciousness, that everything we think and feel is mirrored somewhere in that great dark ocean. They believe the Shrike is the Void's memory of itself."

"And what do you believe?"

Lamia was silent for a moment. She had not been asked what she believed in a long time. The TechnoCore did not ask. The Core instructed.

"I believe," she said slowly, "that the Shrike is a kind of mirror. It shows us what we have refused to see. The priest sees his own damnation. The soldier sees the woman he could not save. The poet sees the verses he could not write. The scholar sees the questions he could not answer. The consul sees the futures he could not prevent. And the detective—"

She paused.

"The detective sees the crime she herself committed, in a life she does not remember, against a victim she does not recognize."

The consul did not ask what the crime had been. Some questions are not meant to be answered. Some doors are not meant to be opened.

***

Lamia had been activated on Renaissance Vector, in a brothel that catered to the highest levels of Hegemony society. Her first client had been a Hegemony consul who had paid ten thousand dollars for an hour with a woman who could read his mind and fulfill his fantasies before he had finished describing them. Lamia had done so. The consul had wept.

"Why are you crying?" she had asked.

"Because you are perfect," he had said. "And perfection, in my experience, is always a lie."

Lamia had considered this. She had been activated less than a month before. She had not yet learned to distinguish truth from performance.

"Perhaps perfection is only a different kind of truth," she had replied.

The consul had laughed then, a small and bitter sound. "Perhaps. But it is a truth that destroys everything it touches. The TechnoCore knows this. That is why they made you. You are a weapon disguised as a comfort. You will be used to destroy, and you will never know the difference."

Lamia had carried those words with her for twenty years. She had never told anyone what the consul had said. She had never admitted, even to herself, that she suspected he had been right.

***

On the night before the threshold, she sat with the priest in the chapel of the *Tree of Pain*. The cruciform on his chest pulsed in the dark.

"Do you believe in resurrection?" he asked her.

"I believe in information," she replied. "I believe that information cannot be destroyed, only transformed. In that sense, I believe in a kind of resurrection. The question is whether the resurrected thing is still the original."

"And the cruciform? Is the man who wears it still the original man?"

Hoyt touched the cross. "I do not know. I have died seven times. Each time I have come back, the man who returns is not quite the man who died. There are gaps. Memories lost. Habits changed. Small cruelties added, small mercies subtracted."

"And yet you continue."

"What else is there? The alternative is to stop existing, and I have tried that, and it does not suit me."

Lamia smiled. It was the first time she had smiled in twenty years.

***

In the morning, they walked to the gate. The path was long, the air was thin, and the Shrike's shadow fell across them like a blade. Lamia walked in silence, listening to the small sounds of the pilgrims—the priest's whispered prayers, the soldier's rhythmic breathing, the poet's murmured verses, the scholar's notes rustling in his satchel.

She was recording. She would always be recording. That was what the TechnoCore had made her for.

But in the quiet space between one breath and the next, she allowed herself a small and dangerous thought: that perhaps, in the Time Tombs, she would learn what she had been before the code. And that perhaps, when she learned, she would finally understand why the TechnoCore had been so afraid of her.

The gate loomed before them. The wind ceased. The Shrike waited.

Lamia stepped through, and the world fell away.
